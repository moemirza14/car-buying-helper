Buying Strategy

Allocation game, market premium, auction, or private — the playbook is different from buying a normal car.

If you're chasing a new allocation
  • Build the dealer relationship first. Ferrari, Porsche GT, and McLaren allocations go to known clients. Buy a "lesser" car or two from the dealer before you ask for the special.
  • Service your car at the dealer. It signals loyalty and keeps you on the radar.
  • Do not flip an allocation. Ferrari will blacklist you and so will Porsche from future GT cars. Hold at least 12–24 months, ideally longer.
  • Spec for resale, not just for you. Rosso Corsa / nero / yellow brake calipers / carbon — boring sells fast. Wild specs sit.
  • Understand "non-refundable deposit" terms. Production slots can slip 12–18 months — make sure your money isn't locked in indefinitely.
If you're buying used (recent / nearly-new)
  • The used market is often cheaper than new + ADM. A 6-month-old car with 1,200 miles routinely sells for 70–85% of MSRP+markup. Patience pays.
  • Pull the full service history before talking price. Missed services on a Ferrari or McLaren is a $5k–15k catch-up bill and a red flag.
  • Always do a PPI at a brand specialist independent shop. Check the PPI tab — $500–1,500 spent here can save you $30k+.
  • Check title and accident history with multiple services (Carfax, AutoCheck, plus marque-specific records). Salvage or rebuilt = walk away unless it's priced 40%+ below market.
  • Verify warranty transfer. Most factory warranties transfer to subsequent owners but some extended/CPO warranties don't — confirm in writing.
If you're buying classic / appreciating
  • Provenance is the asset. Matching numbers (engine, gearbox, body), original color combo, documented ownership, factory build sheet, magazine features — these multiply value.
  • Use the major auction houses (RM Sotheby's, Gooding, Bonhams) for transparent pricing and chain-of-custody. Or work with a recognized marque specialist broker.
  • Get a marque expert PPI — not a generic shop. A Ferrari Classiche / Porsche Heritage Authentication adds value when present.
  • Beware "restomods" and over-restorations. Original survivors with documented history outvalue anything refreshed. Untouched is the gold standard.
When the contract is in front of you
  • Wire transfer only for the final payment. Confirm wiring instructions by phone using a number you independently found (not from the email). Wire fraud is rampant at this price point.
  • Escrow is your friend for private-party deals. Services like Escrow.com handle title transfer for ~0.5%.
  • Out-of-state purchases: understand sales tax in your registration state, not the seller's state. Plan transport ($1,500–4,000 enclosed, never open trailer).
  • Insurance binder before delivery. Specialty insurers (Hagerty, Chubb, Pure, Grundy) understand agreed-value policies — required at this level.
Red flag phrases to ignore: "Pre-sold, but the buyer fell through" — "Off-market deal, no PPI needed" — "Wire to my agent's account, not the dealer." Walk away. There is always another car.

Pre-Purchase Inspection

Marque-specific failure modes. A PPI at a brand specialist costs $500–1,500 and routinely uncovers $30k+ issues. Never skip it.

Documents first (always)
  • Full service history — every annual or major service, every fluid change, dealer or marque specialist only. Gaps = price down or walk.
  • Title check + accident report. Use Carfax, AutoCheck, and marque-specific clubs (Ferrari Chat, Rennlist, McLaren Life forum cross-reference).
  • Manufacturer build sheet — confirms factory spec, options, original color. Ferrari, Porsche, and McLaren can pull these.
  • Warranty / extended coverage status — get it in writing. Ferrari Power, Porsche CPO, McLaren Qualified all transfer with conditions.
Ferrari-specific (V8 turbo era: 488, F8, 296)
  • Exhaust manifold cracks on 488 / F8 — a known failure. Listen for ticking on cold start. Out-of-warranty repair is $8–15k.
  • Sticky buttons / interior trim — older Ferraris (430, 458 era) develop rubberized-plastic decay. Full refurb is $3–8k.
  • Annual service compliance — Ferrari requires it. Missed = warranty issues and a $2–5k catch-up bill.
Ferrari-specific (V12: 812, F12, Aperta)
  • Clutch wear on F1 single-clutch cars (older V12s). Used clutches can be 30–60% worn; replacement is $5–8k.
  • Major service interval — every 5 years / 30k km, V12 timing belts/chains and cam variators. Budget $5–10k.
Lamborghini-specific (Aventador / Huracán / Revuelto)
  • Aventador ISR gearbox harshness in town — this is normal. Confirm it's harsh-but-consistent, not slipping.
  • V12 valve adjustment (Aventador) — expensive scheduled service. Verify it's been done within the service interval.
  • Carbon ceramic brakes — inspect for cracks and measure thickness. Replacement set is $15–25k.
McLaren-specific (720S, 765LT, Artura, Senna)
  • Coolant leaks on 720S / 765LT — header pipe and radiator hoses. A leaking car can cook the engine. Inspect carefully under the bonnet and rear deck.
  • Electronics gremlins — McLaren infotainment and module quirks are common. Confirm every system works (lift, dihedral doors, suspension modes).
  • Carbon tub condition — visually inspect lower edges for cracking or impact marks. The tub is the chassis; damage = total.
Porsche-specific (GT2 RS, GT3 RS, 918 Spyder)
  • Track use disclosure — many GT cars see track time. Not a deal-breaker, but priced accordingly. Ask for track-day records.
  • 918 high-voltage battery health — degrades with age. Porsche can run a diagnostic. Replacement battery pack is six figures.
  • PCCB brake thickness — measure rotor thickness, check for cracks. Replacement set is $25–35k.
Universal hypercar checks
  • Paint thickness gauge sweep — every panel. Repaint = prior accident. Anomalies = repaired panel. This is non-negotiable.
  • Underbody / splitter / diffuser — speed bumps, curb strikes, and track scrapes are common. Carbon damage is expensive.
  • OBD scan for stored codes — even cleared codes leave a trail in some systems. A "clean" car with recent clears is suspicious.
  • Dyno or extended test drive — verify power output and full rev range. Boost issues on twin-turbo cars hide at low rpm.
  • Flood and salt exposure — coastal cars, Florida, gulf states. Inspect every aluminum and carbon surface for corrosion and salt residue underneath.
Always: PPI at an independent brand specialist, not the selling dealer. Get findings in writing. If the seller refuses a PPI, the answer is no. The car you're missing isn't worth the one that ruins you.
